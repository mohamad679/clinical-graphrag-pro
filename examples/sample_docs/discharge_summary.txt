Admitting Diagnosis: Acute non-ST elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI)

History of Present Illness:
Robert Chen is a 67-year-old man with hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and remote tobacco use who presented to the emergency department with 3 hours of substernal chest pressure radiating to the left arm, associated with diaphoresis and mild shortness of breath. EKG on admission showed sinus tachycardia with 1 mm horizontal ST-segment depression in leads V4-V6 and T-wave inversion in leads II, III, and aVF without ST elevation. Initial laboratory evaluation was notable for troponin I 0.8 ng/mL, rising to 2.4 ng/mL at 6 hours, then decreasing to 1.9 ng/mL on hospital day 2, consistent with NSTEMI. Hospital Course:
The patient was admitted to telemetry and treated with aspirin, ticagrelor, and therapeutic anticoagulation during the acute phase. He remained hemodynamically stable without recurrent sustained chest pain after initiation of therapy. High-intensity atorvastatin 80 mg nightly was started. Transthoracic echocardiogram demonstrated mild inferior wall hypokinesis with a left ventricular ejection fraction of 48%, no significant valvular disease, and no pericardial effusion. He was evaluated by cardiology, and the overall presentation was felt to be most consistent with plaque rupture causing NSTEMI. He improved with guideline-directed medical therapy and was ambulating independently by discharge.

Discharge Medications:
